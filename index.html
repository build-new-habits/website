This is what the coach actually sounds like

This is your coach. They don't have a name — but they know yours.

You open the app. Energy at 2.

"I hear you. Energy at 2 is real, and I'm not going to pretend otherwise. Today is for gentle movement only. No pushing. Just being kind to yourself."

You check in and flag that your back is at 7 out of 10 today.

"I'm sorry your back is difficult today. 7 out of 10 is significant, and I want to be really careful with you. I've adjusted everything to protect that area. We'll be gentle."

You tell the coach you're thinking about stopping altogether.

"I'm glad you told me how you're feeling. That takes trust. Can you help me understand what's going on? Whatever's happening, we can figure it out together. But I'm not going to let you give up on yourself without at least talking about it first."

You haven't opened the app in ten days.

"Welcome back. I'm genuinely glad you're here. Whatever kept you away — that's okay. You don't need to explain. Let's start gently and take it one day at a time."

The coach has noticed something across your last few sessions.

"I've noticed you've been running on low for a few days. That's not a failure — it's information. Your nervous system is asking for rest. When you're ready for more, I'll be here."

You tell the coach you've been letting yourself down.

"I want to gently push back on that. I'm watching you try. That's not failure. That's courage."

You've told the coach you're running on empty. The session begins.

"Today's session is gentle by design. There's no rush. Take a breath, and start when you feel ready."

You finish your first full programme.

"I am so proud of you. Take time to really feel this. You committed to something and saw it through. That takes strength."

Behind the magic

Why did the other apps make me feel worse about myself? →

97% of people stop using fitness apps within a year. Research consistently identifies the same cause: shame. Streak-loss, rigid targets, and punishment for absence trigger disengagement — particularly in neurodivergent users and women navigating hormonal change.

— Bondaronek et al., British Journal of Health Psychology, October 2025

Graeme's reflection: I'd quit three fitness apps before I started building this one. They kept telling me to push, and then I got injured and the apps continued to treat me like I was fully fit. They refused to accept I needed adjustment. This is the gap Alongside is trying to close.

What does the science say actually works? →

Burnout is nervous system dysregulation, not tiredness. Pushing through makes it worse. Alongside detects the early signs — sustained low energy, broken sleep, accumulating pain — and responds with restoration, not pressure.

— Dr Claire Plumbly, Burnout: How to Manage Your Nervous System, 2024

Self-compassion improves motivation, not reduces it. Removing shame correlates with stronger resilience, better barrier self-efficacy, and more sustainable physical activity over time.

— Zhang et al., British Journal of Health Psychology, 2025

Graeme's reflection: I built the burnout detection into the app before I read Plumbly's research. When I did read it, I felt seen by a book for the first time in years. I knew the feeling, I just couldn't name it. That's exactly the feeling I want Alongside to give people.

What happens when someone finally pays attention? →

Other-oriented empathy — genuinely caring about others — is protective against burnout. Self-oriented responses, like guilt and distress, are risk factors. Being consistently met with dignity may gradually change how people relate to themselves, and then to others.

— Empathy types and burnout meta-analysis, Psychology, Health & Medicine, November 2025

Graeme's reflection: Being understood changes us. People who feel genuinely seen become, over time, slightly better at seeing others. That's the long horizon Alongside is building toward.
